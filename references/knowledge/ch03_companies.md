# Ch3: Companies

## Summary
Profiles PM culture and interview style at major tech companies: Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Facebook, Apple, and smaller/startup environments. Each company section covers what the PM role looks like there, what they test in interviews, and what makes a strong candidate. **Datedness caveat: This chapter was written in 2014. Company cultures, interview formats, and PM practices have changed significantly. Treat as baseline framing, not current truth.**

## Key Concepts
- **Google**: Data-driven culture, strong engineering bar, "Googleyness" culture fit matters; PM interviews test structured thinking and analytical depth; cross-functional PMs with engineering credibility preferred
- **Microsoft**: PM = "Program Manager" historically; ships-focused; GMAT-style analytical reasoning historically tested; strong emphasis on design and functional spec writing
- **Amazon**: Leadership Principles are the backbone of every interview; behavioral questions map directly to LPs; "customer obsession" is the lens for product decisions; two-pager writing culture
- **Facebook (Meta)**: Speed and impact; "move fast" culture; PMs expected to be data-fluent; product design questions emphasize viral mechanics and social graph thinking
- **Apple**: Secrecy culture; PM role is more execution-focused than strategy; strong design sensibility required; interviews tend to be less structured than Google/Facebook
- **Startups**: Broader scope, more ambiguity, closer to the CEO; interviewers test scrappiness, ownership, and bias to action over process
- **Common pattern across all**: Every top company tests (a) product sense, (b) analytical/data ability, (c) execution, (d) communication, (e) culture fit — the weights differ, not the categories

## Direct Quotes / Signals Worth Preserving
- Amazon's LP framing: "We want to hire leaders, not just product managers" — every behavioral answer should map to a named LP
- Google: "Googleyness" is real — avoid arrogance, show intellectual curiosity, collaborate rather than win arguments
- The book flags that Amazon's interview is the most consistently LP-structured; this has only intensified since 2014
