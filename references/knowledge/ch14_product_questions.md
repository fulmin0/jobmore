# Ch14: Product Questions

## Summary
The largest and most practically important chapter for PM interviews. Covers product design questions, product improvement questions, product strategy questions, and metric/success questions. The "user-first framework" is the canonical structure for product design answers. High-density chapter — every major question type has a named framework.

## Key Concepts

### Product Design Questions ("Design a product for X")
- **User-first framework** (the canonical structure):
  1. **Clarify the goal**: What are we optimizing for? Who is the target user? Any constraints?
  2. **Define the user**: Who are they? (demographics, behaviors, context of use) — pick ONE user persona and go deep, don't try to serve everyone
  3. **User goals / pain points**: What is the user trying to accomplish? What's getting in their way?
  4. **Prioritize pain points**: Which pain point is biggest / most frequent / most underserved?
  5. **Brainstorm solutions**: Generate 3–5 ideas; briefly describe each
  6. **Prioritize solutions**: Pick 1–2 based on user impact, feasibility, and strategic fit
  7. **Summarize and tradeoffs**: What would you build? What are you NOT building and why?
  8. **Success metrics**: How would you know if this is working?
- **Common design question traps**:
  - Jumping to the solution before defining the user
  - Serving multiple user types at once instead of picking one
  - "Adding features" as the default answer — the best design answers often simplify
  - Not naming tradeoffs — every product decision involves a tradeoff
- **Designing for accessibility / underserved users**: Deliberately choosing a non-obvious user (elderly, non-English speaker, low-income) often leads to more creative and more defensible answers

### Product Improvement Questions ("How would you improve X?")
- **Structure**: Clarify → Current state analysis → User segments → Pain points → Prioritized improvements → Metrics
- **Don't start with "add a feature"**: Start with "what are users trying to do, and what's preventing them?" — the improvement follows from the problem
- **Use data to anchor**: "If I had data, I'd look at..." — shows analytical instinct even when data isn't provided
- **Competitive framing**: "I'd also look at how [competitor] handles this, because..." — shows market awareness

### Product Strategy Questions ("Should we build X?", "What's your product vision for Y?")
- **Strategic framework**:
  1. Market opportunity: Is there a real user problem here? How large?
  2. Competitive position: Do we have an advantage? Who else plays here?
  3. Strategic fit: Does this align with the company's strengths and long-term bets?
  4. Build vs. buy vs. partner
  5. Risks and mitigations
- **"Should we build X?" structure**: Problem size → Our fit → Cost/risk → Recommendation (clear, one sentence)

### Metrics / Success Questions ("How would you measure success for X?")
- **Framework**:
  1. Clarify the goal (acquisition? engagement? retention? revenue?)
  2. Define the primary metric (the "north star")
  3. Define supporting metrics (guardrails: don't optimize primary at the expense of these)
  4. Define counter-metrics (what we're NOT trying to move)
  5. Leading vs. lagging indicators
- **North Star metric principles**:
  - Measures actual user value delivered (not just activity)
  - Correlates with long-term business health
  - Is actionable (the team can influence it)
  - Example: Airbnb = nights booked; Spotify = time listening; Slack = messages sent in a month

### Common Question Types Summary
| Question Type | Lead With | Key Test |
|---|---|---|
| Design a product | User definition | Do you start with user or feature? |
| Improve a product | Current pain points | Do you diagnose before prescribing? |
| Product strategy | Market + fit | Can you reason about build vs. buy? |
| Success metrics | North star definition | Do you understand leading vs. lagging? |
| Favorite product | Why it works, not what it does | Can you articulate product principles? |

## Direct Quotes / Signals Worth Preserving
- "Never start with the solution. Start with the user. Every time."
- On favorite product questions: "Don't describe the product — explain WHY it works. What problem does it solve? Why is this the right solution? What would you improve?"
- On metrics: "The north star should measure value delivered to the user, not activity on the platform. Messages sent ≠ value. Messages that received a reply ≠ value. Problems solved = value."
- "A great product design answer makes one clear choice about the user and goes deep. A weak answer tries to serve everyone and says nothing interesting about anyone."

## Relevance to Job Search Pipeline
- **Interview prep for active pipeline jobs**: For every job in `recruiter_screen` or `interview`, prepare at least one product design answer for the company's core product using the user-first framework
- **Job Intelligence product research**: The company's core product analyzed in `job_intelligence.md` gives you the material for "improve this product" answers in the interview
- **Favorite product question prep**: Pick 2–3 products you can speak to deeply — not your most-used product, but the one you have the most interesting point of view on
- **Metrics fluency**: For every role in the pipeline, know the likely north star metric for the product — this tells you what the PM team optimizes for
