# Ch1: Introduction

## Summary
Brief orientation to the book's purpose: helping candidates get PM jobs at top tech companies. Covers who the book is for (new grads, engineers transitioning, experienced PMs, non-tech people) and what the PM role broadly entails. Sets the framing that PM interviews test judgment, communication, and structured thinking — not just knowledge.

## Key Concepts
- **Audience segments**: New grad, engineer transitioning, experienced PM, non-tech background — each has different gaps to address
- **What interviewers actually assess**: Product sense, analytical ability, communication, execution, and culture fit
- **Book structure**: Covers getting experience, the interview process, resume/cover letter, then each interview question type (behavioral, estimation, product, case, coding)
- **Core PM premise**: PMs don't manage people directly; they drive product outcomes through influence

## Direct Quotes / Signals Worth Preserving
- The book acknowledges this is a 2014 snapshot; company-specific culture chapters (Google, Microsoft, Amazon, etc.) should be treated as baseline context, not current truth
- The authors (McDowell + Bavaro) frame the PM role as combining business acumen, technical depth, and user empathy — "the glue between design, engineering, and business"

- Use as orientation only; the real value is in Ch2 (PM Role), Ch11 (Define Yourself), Ch12 (Behavioral), Ch14 (Product), Ch17 (Appendix)
