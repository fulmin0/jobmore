## Key Concepts

### Ian McAllister: "What Distinguishes the Top 1% of Product Managers from the Top 10%?"

The framework that defines senior PM thinking. The top 10% execute well. The top 1% do all of that AND have these distinguishing traits:

| Trait | Top 10% Behavior | Top 1% Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| **Think Big** | Optimize the current product | Ask "what if we reimagined the whole thing?" |
| **Communicate** | Clear updates to the team | Creates shared clarity across the org; writing that moves decisions |
| **Simplify** | Ships good features | Relentlessly removes complexity; says no to good ideas |
| **Prioritize** | Has a roadmap | Makes hard tradeoff calls with conviction; explains why in writing |
| **Forecast & Measure** | Defines success metrics | Predicts outcomes and validates; adjusts strategy based on data |
| **Execute** | Ships on time | Identifies and removes blockers before they become crises |
| **Technical Trade-offs** | Understands feasibility | Has an opinion on architecture choices; earns engineering respect |
| **Design** | Reviews designs | Has aesthetic judgment and product taste; shapes design direction |
| **Copy** | Writes clear specs | Understands the power of naming, framing, and word choice in UX |

**Key insight**: The gap between top 10% and top 1% is not effort or intelligence — it's **ambition of thought** (Think Big), **clarity of communication** (Communicate, Copy), and **judgment about what to kill** (Simplify, Prioritize).

### Ken Norton: "How to Hire a Product Manager"

6 criteria for PM hiring, in order of importance:

1. **Raw intelligence** — "Most importantly, raw intellectual horsepower. I'll take a wickedly smart, inexperienced PM over one of average intellect and years of experience any day." Problem-solving ability above all else.
2. **Technical background** — Not the ability to code, but enough depth to earn engineering trust, understand tradeoffs, and have technical conversations without being hand-held
3. **Spidey-sense / Product instincts** — The gut that says "this is the right call" before the data confirms it. Built from deep user empathy and pattern recognition across products
4. **Earned leadership** — Authority that comes from being right, being prepared, and being trusted — not from title or tenure
5. **Multiple perspectives** — Can hold the user's view, the business view, the engineering view, and the strategic view simultaneously — and synthesize them into a decision
6. **Shipped something** — Has pushed something to production and lived with the consequences. The shipping experience teaches tradeoffs that no class, book, or spec can

**Key insight**: Norton explicitly de-emphasizes process and credentials. A PM who has shipped real things and thinks hard beats an MBA who knows every framework.

### Adam Nash: "What Does a Product Manager Actually Do?"

Three core responsibilities — deceptively simple, endlessly debatable in practice:

1. **Strategy** — "Quite simply, it's the product manager's job to articulate two simple things: What game are we playing? How do we keep score?" If the team doesn't have crisp answers to both questions, that's a PM failure.
2. **Prioritization** — Given the strategy and the score, what do we build next? Why? This is where most PM judgment lives — not in having good ideas, but in knowing which idea to execute now vs. later vs. never.
3. **Execution** — Getting it shipped. Not just managing the delivery, but removing blockers, making the calls that unblock others, and ensuring the shipped product is the intended product.

**Key insight**: "What game are we playing? How do we keep score?" is the two-question test for whether a product has a real strategy. Use it to audit any product work — if you can't answer both in one sentence each, the strategy is unclear.

### Sachin Rekhi: "Roadmap Inputs"

Framework for what should feed a product roadmap — where ideas and priorities come from:

| Input Source | What It Surfaces |
|---|---|
| **Usage metrics** | What users actually do (vs. what they say); where they drop off; what they return to |
| **User interviews** | Unmet needs, workarounds, emotional context that metrics can't capture |
| **Customer feedback** | Support tickets, NPS verbatims, feature requests — the loudest signal, not always the most important |
| **Competition** | What alternatives exist; where competitors are investing; gaps in the market |
| **Internal innovation** | Eng/design ideas from the people closest to the code and the craft |
| **Audience surveys** | Quantitative validation at scale for hypotheses surfaced by interviews |

**Key insight**: Customer feedback (the loudest input) is often the least reliable guide for strategy. Usage metrics and user interviews together are more predictive. The best roadmaps balance all six — over-indexing on any one source creates bias.

**Application**: Before building a roadmap, map each major initiative back to which input source(s) drove it. A roadmap built entirely on customer feature requests is a reactive roadmap. A roadmap with no customer input is disconnected.

### Amazon's 14 Leadership Principles

The foundational framework for all behavioral interview answers at Amazon. Every answer should map to one or more LPs.

| # | Principle | Core Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Customer Obsession** | Start with the customer and work backwards. Not competitor-first or technology-first. |
| 2 | **Ownership** | Act like an owner. Don't say "that's not my job." Think long-term, not just this quarter. |
| 3 | **Invent and Simplify** | Create new approaches. Look for simple. Complexity is a tax on the organization. |
| 4 | **Are Right, A Lot** | Have strong judgment. Seek diverse perspectives to stress-test your thinking. |
| 5 | **Learn and Be Curious** | Never stop learning. Pursue self-improvement. Explore new possibilities. |
| 6 | **Hire and Develop the Best** | Raise the bar with every hire. Coach and stretch your people. |
| 7 | **Insist on the Highest Standards** | High standards are contagious. Push back on "good enough." |
| 8 | **Think Big** | Small thinking is a self-fulfilling prophecy. Create and communicate a bold direction. |
| 9 | **Bias for Action** | Speed matters. Many decisions are reversible. Don't wait for perfect information. |
| 10 | **Frugality** | Do more with less. Constraints breed creativity. |
| 11 | **Earn Trust** | Listen attentively, speak candidly, treat others respectfully. Admit mistakes. |
| 12 | **Dive Deep** | Stay connected to the details. Audit frequently. Skeptical when metrics and anecdotes disagree. |
| 13 | **Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit** | Challenge decisions respectfully. Once a decision is made, commit fully. |
| 14 | **Deliver Results** | Focus on key inputs and deliver them on time. Don't accept mediocre outputs. |

**LP Mapping for PM behavioral answers**:
- "Tell me about a time you influenced without authority" → Earn Trust + Have Backbone
- "Tell me about a hard prioritization call" → Are Right, A Lot + Customer Obsession + Bias for Action
- "Tell me about a product decision that didn't work" → Learn and Be Curious + Dive Deep + Deliver Results
- "Tell me about a time you simplified something" → Invent and Simplify + Insist on the Highest Standards
- "Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager" → Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit

- "Quite simply, it's the product manager's job to articulate two simple things: What game are we playing? How do we keep score?"

## Relevance to Job Search Pipeline

- **Universal prep**: McAllister's Top 1% framework is the lens for every senior PM application — resume content, elevator pitch, behavioral stories should all demonstrate at least 3–4 of the 9 traits explicitly
- **Amazon applications**: Map every behavioral story to an LP before the interview. Prepare at least one story per LP for LPs 1–5, 8, 9, 13, 14 — these are the most commonly tested in PM loops
- **"What game are we playing?"**: Use Nash's two-question test in every Job Intelligence package — can you state the product's strategy in one sentence and the success metric in one sentence? If not, the JI is incomplete
- **Roadmap conversation prep**: Rekhi's input framework is the answer to "How do you build a roadmap?" in any interview — present all six sources, show how you weight them, give a real example
- **Story bank tagging**: In `data/story_bank.md`, tag each STAR story with the LP(s) it maps to — enables fast retrieval for Amazon interview prep
- **McAllister trait check before submitting any application**:
  - Resume bullets show Simplify (what you killed) and Forecast & Measure (what you predicted and validated)
  - Elevator pitch shows Think Big (what you reimagined) and Communicate (crisp framing)
  - Cover letter shows Prioritize (why this role over others, with conviction)
