# Ch6: Behind the Interview Scenes

## Summary
Explains the PM hiring process from the interviewer's side — how resumes are screened, what interviewers are actually evaluating, how debrief decisions get made, and what common hiring mistakes look like. Insider knowledge for candidates. **Datedness caveat: Specific process details (phone screens, loop structure) vary by company and have evolved since 2014. The evaluation criteria and debrief dynamics are more durable.**

## Key Concepts
- **Resume screen (15-second rule)**: Recruiters skim, not read. A resume that fails the 15-second scan doesn't get a phone screen — even if the candidate is qualified
- **Phone screen objectives**: Recruiter screen = "Is this person worth an engineer's time?" Technical/PM screen = "Can they think clearly under pressure? Do they have product instincts?"
- **The interview loop**:
  - Typically 4–6 rounds at large companies
  - Each interviewer is assigned a dimension (product sense, analytical, behavioral, technical) — they are not all testing the same thing
  - One interviewer is usually the "bar raiser" or "skeptic" — their job is to poke holes
- **Debrief dynamics**:
  - Hiring is often consensus-driven; one strong "no" can block an offer
  - "Strong hire" signals need to come from at least one interviewer — a stack of "lean yes" doesn't get through
  - Concrete stories and data > vague positive impressions
  - Interviewers write notes immediately after; candidates who communicate clearly leave better notes
- **What kills offers at debrief**:
  - No concrete examples (all process, no story)
  - Arrogance signals (taking full credit, dismissing teammates)
  - Weak answers on dimension the interviewer was assigned to evaluate
  - Culture fit concerns from the "lunch round" or informal conversation
- **How interviewers decide "product sense"**: They are testing whether you approach problems from the user's perspective first, or from features/technology first
- **Feedback loops**: Most companies do not give substantive feedback to rejected candidates — but feedback given informally (recruiter debrief call) is gold; always ask

## Direct Quotes / Signals Worth Preserving
- On debrief: "One strong hire can pull a lean yes over the line. One strong no can block an offer. Know who the skeptic in your loop is."
- On 15-second rule: "A resume isn't read; it's skimmed. You have about 15 seconds to make your case."
- On culture fit: "The lunch round isn't just lunch. Everything you say is being evaluated."

## Relevance to Job Search Pipeline
- **Resume content**: The 15-second rule drives the headline structure — lead with impact metrics and company names that create instant recognition
- **Interview prep**: Knowing that each interviewer owns a dimension means you should map your stories to dimensions, not just questions
- **Post-interview**: Always send a follow-up thank-you; it's the last chance to reinforce the strong-hire signal
- **Behavioral prep**: "No concrete examples" is the #1 killer at debrief — every answer must end with a specific outcome, not a general statement
