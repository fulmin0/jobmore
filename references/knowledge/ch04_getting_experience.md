# Ch4: Getting the Right Experience

## Summary
Covers how to build PM credentials when you don't have a traditional PM background — for engineers, MBAs, non-tech people, and career switchers. Focuses on side projects, internal transfers, APM programs, and how to frame existing experience as PM-relevant. Practical playbook for candidates who feel under-qualified.

## Key Concepts
- **The "PM experience paradox"**: You can't get a PM job without PM experience, but you can't get PM experience without a PM job — the chapter's entire purpose is breaking this loop
- **Paths in**:
  1. APM programs (Google, Microsoft, Facebook, Twitter, Yahoo) — structured entry for new grads
  2. Internal transfer — move from eng/design/ops/data into PM at current company
  3. Side project / app — ship something real; the product itself is evidence
  4. Startup founding or early employee — broad scope counts as PM experience even without the title
  5. MBA → PM — works best at companies with strong MBA hiring pipelines (Amazon, Microsoft historically)
- **How to frame non-PM experience**:
  - Engineering: emphasize product decisions made, user-facing work, cross-functional coordination
  - Design: emphasize user research, prioritization tradeoffs, business case work
  - Marketing/ops: emphasize data analysis, customer insight, go-to-market ownership
  - Consulting: emphasize problem structuring, client management, decision framing
- **Side project requirements to be credible**: Must be shipped and live (not a prototype), must have real users or measurable engagement, must demonstrate a product decision you made and why
- **What makes a PM internship count**: Owned a spec, shipped a feature, made a tradeoff decision — not just attended standups
- **"Showing you can do the job"**: The underlying test for experience-building is: does this prove you've done the actual work of a PM (define, prioritize, ship, measure)?

## Direct Quotes / Signals Worth Preserving
- "The best way to become a PM is to be a PM" — the chapter then spends 50 pages explaining how to fake your way to that first role (legitimately)
- On side projects: "If you built an app, it needs to be real. A prototype that only you've used doesn't count."
- On internal transfers: "The easiest path to a PM role is often through your current company — you already have credibility, and they already trust you"

## Relevance to Job Search Pipeline
- **Resume content**: Use this chapter's "framing" section when translating non-PM roles into PM-relevant bullets — especially for cross-industry candidates
- **Cover letter "why PM?" story**: The origin story of how you got into PM (internal transfer, side project, etc.) is your best cover letter opening hook
- **APM program awareness**: If targeting Google/Meta/Microsoft APM programs, the framing here (ship something, show impact, structure thinking) still applies
- **Experience files enrichment**: When writing `data/experience/{company}_{role}.md` for non-PM roles, use this chapter's framing to identify which bullets to surface as PM-signal
