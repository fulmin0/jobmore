# Elevator Pitch Rubric

**Source**: Ch11 (Define Yourself / Personal Narrative), Ch12 (Nugget First), Ch17 (McAllister: Think Big + Communicate traits)
**Format**: LinkedIn DM / InMail to a hiring manager — ~4 short paragraphs, plain prose, no bullets.
**Perspective**: HM who gets 20 cold DMs a week. They're deciding in 10 seconds whether this person is worth a reply.

---

## How to Apply

One pass — read the pitch cold as the HM would. Then score each dimension.

A score of 1 in any dimension caps the overall at 3.0 maximum.

---

## Cold-Read Check (Before Scoring)

Read the pitch as if you don't know this person. Answer:
- Do you know within the first sentence what this person does and why they're reaching out?
- Is there a specific, named reason why *this company* vs. any other?
- Can you tell what the person is actually asking for?
- Does it read like a human wrote it, or like a template was filled in?
- Would *you* reply to this?

---

## Dimension Scoring

| # | Dimension | Weight |
|---|-----------|--------|
| 1 | Hook & Clarity of Purpose | 25% |
| 2 | Specificity of Company Connection | 25% |
| 3 | Credibility Signal | 25% |
| 4 | Ask & Tone | 15% |
| 5 | Distinctiveness | 10% |

---

### 1. Hook & Clarity of Purpose (25%)

Does the opening sentence tell the HM who you are and why you're writing — without making them work for it?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 5 | First sentence delivers: role/level + one concrete signal of credibility + reason for reaching out. Reader knows exactly who this is and why within 10 words. Nugget First — the punchline is in the opening, not buried in paragraph 3. |
| 4 | Clear opening that identifies the person and the intent. May take 2 sentences to land. No ambiguity about what's being asked. |
| 3 | Purpose is clear by end of first paragraph. Opening is slightly slow or generic ("I came across your profile and..."). |
| 2 | Has to read to paragraph 2 or 3 to understand why the person is writing. Opening is a preamble. |
| 1 | Unclear what the person wants. Could be a sales pitch, a networking request, or a job application — reader can't tell. |

**What separates 3 → 4**: No warm-up sentences. The pitch starts at the point, not before it.

**What separates 4 → 5**: The opening sentence would make the HM think "this person gets it" — not just "this person is a PM looking for a job."

---

### 2. Specificity of Company Connection (25%)

Does the pitch name *something specific* about this company — product, strategy, recent launch, market bet — and connect it to a real experience?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 5 | Names a specific product, strategic initiative, or market position. Connects it to a concrete experience or belief the sender holds. The connection is non-generic — it couldn't appear in a pitch to a competitor. |
| 4 | Names something specific and connects it to experience. Connection is genuine but could be slightly stronger or more personal. |
| 3 | Names the company's general space or mission. Connection is real but vague ("your focus on AI in retail aligns with my work in AI"). |
| 2 | Uses the company name but the pitch could be sent to any company in the same sector with minor edits. No specific product or initiative named. |
| 1 | Generic. "I'm passionate about [Company]'s mission to [mission statement]." No research visible. |

**Red flags**: "I am passionate about [Company]" with no evidence. Mission statement quoted verbatim. No specific product named.

**From the book**: "A cover letter that says 'I am passionate about technology' tells me nothing. A cover letter that says 'I read the post-IPO shareholder letter and your bet on X aligns with my work on Y' tells me everything." Same rule applies here.

---

### 3. Credibility Signal (25%)

Does the pitch give the HM a reason to take the sender seriously — one concrete signal that says "this person has done this before at scale"?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 5 | One sharp, specific accomplishment or credential that is directly relevant to this role. Scope is explicit (team size, user count, revenue, or company name that carries weight). Not a list of accomplishments — one well-chosen signal. |
| 4 | Strong credibility signal with clear relevance. May be slightly less specific on scope. |
| 3 | Credibility is established but via a general claim rather than a specific accomplishment ("I've led AI product teams" vs. "I shipped the AI ordering assistant that drove 20% conversion uplift across 400+ pharmacy locations"). |
| 2 | Credibility relies on credentials alone (company names, years of experience) without any outcome signal. |
| 1 | No credibility signal. Just a self-description ("I'm a passionate and results-driven PM"). |

**What separates 3 → 4**: The accomplishment is specific enough that the HM can picture it — a real product, a real outcome, a real scale.

**What separates 4 → 5**: The accomplishment is *relevant to the HM's specific problem or JD* — not just impressive in the abstract. McAllister's Forecast & Measure trait: shows that you predicted an outcome and then validated it.

---

### 4. Ask & Tone (15%)

Is the ask clear, appropriately scoped, and confident without being presumptuous?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 5 | Ask is specific and scoped: a 20-minute call, a coffee, or a conversation about the role — not "any advice you have" or "looking for opportunities." Tone is confident and peer-to-peer, not deferential. Doesn't grovel or over-apologize for reaching out. |
| 4 | Clear ask. Tone is good. Slight over-hedging ("if you have any time") but recoverable. |
| 3 | Ask exists but is vague ("open to connecting"). Tone is appropriate but lacks confidence. |
| 2 | No clear ask — leaves it up to the HM to figure out what's wanted. Or ask is too big ("happy to chat about any open roles"). |
| 1 | Passive, apologetic, or entitled. "I hope you don't mind me reaching out" or "I'd be a great fit and would love to join." |

**Red flags**: "I hope to hear from you" (passive). "I'd be a perfect fit" (presumptuous). "Let me know if anything comes up" (no ask). Over-long close that thanks the person for their time before they've given it.

---

### 5. Distinctiveness (10%)

Would this pitch stand out among 20 cold DMs the HM received this week — or does it sound like every other pitch?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 5 | Something in the pitch is genuinely memorable — an unexpected connection, a specific insight about the company's product, or a framing that shows how the sender thinks. McAllister's Think Big trait: the sender doesn't just present their resume, they show a perspective. |
| 4 | Pitch is well-written and specific. Not generic, but not particularly surprising either. |
| 3 | Solid pitch that follows a good template. Competent but forgettable. |
| 2 | Follows a recognizable formula. Could have been written by anyone. |
| 1 | Generic. Template-feeling. No original thought visible. |

**What separates 3 → 4**: One sentence in the pitch that the HM didn't expect — a specific observation, a sharp point of view, or an unexpected connection.

**What separates 4 → 5**: The HM finishes reading and thinks "this person has thought about us" — not just "this person is a qualified PM."

---

## Scoring

```
Weighted total = (D1 × 0.25) + (D2 × 0.25) + (D3 × 0.25) + (D4 × 0.15) + (D5 × 0.10)
```

| Score | Assessment |
|-------|------------|
| Below 2.5 | Would not get a reply — too generic or unclear |
| 2.5 – 3.0 | Might get a reply on a good day; competes poorly against targeted pitches |
| 3.0 – 3.5 | Solid — gets replies from engaged HMs |
| 3.5 – 4.0 | Strong — stands out in a crowded inbox |
| 4.0+ | Exceptional — HM forwards it to the team before replying |

---

## Common Failure Patterns

- **Warm-up sentences**: "I hope this message finds you well" / "I came across your profile" — cut everything before the actual first point
- **Generic company connection**: Paraphrasing the company's LinkedIn About page is not company research
- **Accomplishment list**: Pitching 3–4 accomplishments dilutes focus — one sharp signal beats four vague ones
- **Passive close**: "I'd welcome any feedback you have" — says nothing, asks for nothing
- **Over-length**: If it doesn't fit in a LinkedIn DM preview without scrolling, it's too long
- **Superpower absent**: Can't answer "why you over another strong PM?" in 30 seconds — the pitch should implicitly answer this
