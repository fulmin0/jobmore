# Ch10: Company Research

## Summary
How to research a company before an interview — what sources to use, what to look for, and how to use the research in interviews and cover letters. Covers both publicly available sources and less-obvious signals. Directly applicable to the Job Intelligence workflow.

## Key Concepts
- **Why research matters**: Interviewers can tell in the first 2 minutes whether a candidate has done homework. Product design questions asked about the company's own products require knowing the products.
- **Research dimensions**:
  1. **Product**: What do they build? Who uses it? What's the core value proposition? What's the monetization model?
  2. **Business**: Revenue model, funding stage, recent earnings (if public), major strategic moves (acquisitions, pivots, market expansion)
  3. **Culture**: Glassdoor, Blind, LinkedIn — but also the company blog, engineering blog, PM blog posts from employees
  4. **Competitors**: Who are they competing with? How are they positioned? What does winning look like?
  5. **People**: Who's the hiring manager? Who are the interviewers? LinkedIn profiles tell you what they've shipped and care about
- **Sources to use**:
  - Company website / about page / investor relations
  - Recent press releases and tech press (TechCrunch, The Information, Protocol)
  - Engineering and product blogs (Medium company publications, Substack newsletters by employees)
  - Glassdoor for culture/interview reports
  - LinkedIn for interviewer backgrounds
  - Product Hunt for recent launches
  - App Store / Play Store reviews for user sentiment on consumer products
- **How to use research in interviews**:
  - Product design questions: reference the real product, real users, real metrics (DAU, subscription model, etc.)
  - "Why this company?" — name a specific product decision or strategic move and explain why it's interesting
  - Behavioral questions: show you know what the company's PM culture values (e.g., Amazon = LPs, Google = data, startup = speed)
- **Common research failure modes**:
  - Knowing the company name and funding stage but nothing about the actual product
  - Citing outdated information (check dates on all sources)
  - Over-praising without specifics ("I love what you're doing with AI" when you can't name what)

## Direct Quotes / Signals Worth Preserving
- "If you can't describe their top product and its core user journey in two sentences, you haven't done enough research."
- On interviewer research: "Knowing what your interviewers have shipped is one of the most underused interview advantages."
- On competition: "Knowing who they compete with — and having a point of view on the competitive landscape — shows strategic thinking before the interview even starts."

## Relevance to Job Search Pipeline
- **Job Intelligence workflow** (`prompts/job_intelligence.md`): This chapter is the manual version of what `job_intelligence.md` systematizes — product/business/culture/competition research dimensions map directly to the JI sections
- **Cover letter hook**: Company research (specific product, recent launch, strategic bet) is the source of the hook paragraph in every cover letter
- **Elevator pitch**: The "why this company" section of the elevator pitch draws from research dimensions #1 (product) and #2 (business)
- **Interview prep**: Before any phone screen, verify you can describe the top product's value prop and monetization model in two sentences
