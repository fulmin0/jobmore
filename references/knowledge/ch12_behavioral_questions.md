# Ch12: Behavioral Questions

## Summary
The full framework for answering behavioral interview questions — the STAR/SAR method, the "Nugget First" technique, common question categories, and how to prepare a story bank. One of the highest-value chapters. The frameworks here are directly applicable to any PM behavioral interview at any company.

## Key Concepts
- **STAR framework**:
  - **S**ituation: Set the context (brief — 1–2 sentences)
  - **T**ask: What was your specific responsibility?
  - **A**ction: What did YOU do? (focus on your actions, not the team's)
  - **R**esult: What changed? (quantify where possible)
  - SAR = same thing, Situation + Action + Result (Task folded in)
- **Nugget First technique**: Lead your answer with the conclusion or theme, then tell the story. Example: "A time I influenced without authority — this happened when I was at [company] and had to get eng to deprioritize their roadmap for a user safety issue..." The nugget orients the listener before the story.
- **Common behavioral categories** (with the sub-question patterns):
  1. **Leadership / Influence without authority**: "Tell me about a time you led a team without being the manager"
  2. **Conflict resolution**: "Tell me about a disagreement with an engineer / stakeholder / PM"
  3. **Failure and learning**: "Tell me about a product decision that didn't go as planned"
  4. **Prioritization under pressure**: "Tell me about a time you had to cut scope or say no"
  5. **Data-driven decision making**: "Tell me about a time you used data to change direction"
  6. **Ambiguity and 0-to-1**: "Tell me about a time you worked in an undefined space with no clear requirements"
  7. **Customer focus**: "Tell me about a time you advocated for the user against internal pressure"
  8. **Cross-functional execution**: "Tell me about a time you shipped something through a complex org"
- **Story bank preparation**: Build 8–12 stories before the interview loop. Each story should be flexible enough to answer multiple question types — a single story about a product failure can answer failure, prioritization, and data questions depending on emphasis.
- **Weakness answer structure**: (1) Name the real weakness, (2) describe a specific moment it showed up, (3) explain the mitigation system you built, (4) show it's under control (ideally with evidence)
- **"What's your biggest accomplishment?" structure**: Not the most impressive outcome — the one with the most interesting decision-making journey. Outcome + decision + what you'd do differently.
- **Common behavioral interview failures**:
  - "We" answers: "We shipped X" — interviewer can't tell what you did. Always say "I"
  - No result: Story ends with the action, not the outcome
  - Too long: More than 3–4 minutes per answer is a signal of poor communication
  - Vague: "I helped improve the process" — what process, how, measured how?
  - No reflection: Strong candidates show learning from their stories, not just success

## Direct Quotes / Signals Worth Preserving
- On "we" answers: "Use 'I' not 'we' — the interviewer is trying to understand what YOU did, not what the team did. If you struggle to separate your contribution from the team's, that's information too."
- On Nugget First: "Start with the punchline. Then tell the story. Most candidates bury the lead."
- On story bank: "Prepare 8–10 stories. Make sure each one can be stretched to answer at least 3 different question types. Breadth of material, not depth of any one story."
- On failure questions: "The best failure answers don't minimize the failure — they show you understood exactly what went wrong and why, and you'd do it differently now."
