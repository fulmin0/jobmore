# Cover Letter Rubric

**Source**: Ch9 (Cover Letters), Ch11 (Why This Company), Ch17 (McAllister: Communicate + Prioritize traits)
**Format**: LaTeX, compiled to PDF — 3 paragraphs, one page maximum.
**Perspective**: HM who has already seen the resume and is deciding whether to move this candidate to a phone screen.

---

## Key Difference from the Elevator Pitch

The elevator pitch is a cold outreach — the HM doesn't know you. The cover letter arrives with the resume — the HM has already formed an impression. The cover letter's job is to explain what the resume *can't*: why this company specifically, why now, and what you bring that the bullets don't make obvious.

If the cover letter repeats resume bullets in paragraph form, it has failed its only job.

---

## How to Apply

One pass — read the cover letter immediately after the resume, as an HM would. Then score each dimension.

A score of 1 in any dimension caps the overall at 3.0 maximum.

---

## Cold-Read Check (Before Scoring)

Read the cover letter right after reading the resume. Answer:
- Did you learn anything new that wasn't in the resume?
- Can you identify one specific thing that explains *why this company*?
- Does the tone match the company's culture (startup = direct and bold; large company = measured and evidence-led)?
- Is it one page? Does it feel like the right length or padded?
- Would this make an HM more confident or less confident in the candidate?

## Dimension Scoring

| # | Dimension | Weight |
|---|-----------|--------|
| 1 | Hook: Why This Company | 30% |
| 2 | Evidence Paragraph: Relevance & Additionality | 30% |
| 3 | Non-Repetition of Resume | 20% |
| 4 | Tone & Close | 10% |
| 5 | Format & Length Discipline | 10% |

---

### 1. Hook: Why This Company (30%)

The first paragraph must answer: why this company, why this role, why now. It must be specific — not aspirational.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 5 | Names a specific product, strategic initiative, market bet, or recent launch. Connects it to a concrete personal experience or belief — not generic enthusiasm. Could not be copied to a cover letter for a competitor. Shows the sender has done real research. |
| 4 | Specific company connection with genuine personal link. Slightly less sharp than a 5 — maybe the connection is real but not perfectly targeted to the JD's priorities. |
| 3 | Names the company's space or general mission. Connection is real but vague. Could apply to several companies in the same sector. |
| 2 | Uses the company name but no specific product, initiative, or market insight. Opens with enthusiasm rather than evidence. |
| 1 | Generic opening. "I am writing to apply for the Product Manager role at [Company]." Could be submitted to any company. |

**Hard rule**: "I am passionate about [Company]" without evidence of what specifically — automatic 1 on this dimension.

**From the book**: "A cover letter that says 'I am passionate about technology' tells me nothing. A cover letter that says 'I read the post-IPO shareholder letter and your bet on X aligns with my work on Y' tells me everything."

**Source for hook content**: `job_intelligence.md` — the company research section has the specific products, strategic priorities, and HM signals to draw from. If the hook could have been written without reading `job_intelligence.md`, it wasn't specific enough.

---

### 2. Evidence Paragraph: Relevance & Additionality (30%)

The second paragraph should present 2–3 accomplishments that map directly to the JD's biggest requirements. These should add context the resume can't provide, or spotlight bullets that deserve more attention than a 150-char resume line allows.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 5 | 2–3 accomplishments, each directly mapped to a named JD pillar. At least one accomplishment includes context the resume can't carry (scale of decision, constraint faced, approach taken). Bullets are not repeated verbatim — they're contextualized. |
| 4 | Strong accomplishment selection with JD alignment. Mostly additive — very minor overlap with resume. |
| 3 | Accomplishments are relevant to the role but the mapping to specific JD pillars is implicit, not explicit. Reader has to make the connection. |
| 2 | Accomplishments are impressive but not specifically chosen for this JD. Generic PM signal without targeted fit. |
| 1 | Accomplishments are lifted directly from the resume with minor wording changes. Adds nothing. |

**What separates 3 → 4**: Each accomplishment in the paragraph could be followed by "which is why I'm the right person for [specific JD requirement]" and it would make sense.

**What separates 4 → 5**: One accomplishment shows what the resume couldn't — the decision behind the number, the constraint that made it hard, or the approach that was non-obvious. McAllister's Communicate trait: creates shared clarity, not just information transfer.

**Gating rule**: The evidence paragraph must be written after the resume content is approved — the bullets chosen here should match the approved resume framing, not contradict it.

---

### 3. Non-Repetition of Resume (20%)

Every sentence in the cover letter should be doing work the resume can't do. If a sentence could be deleted and replaced with "see my resume," delete it.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 5 | Zero sentence is a paraphrase of a resume bullet. Every sentence adds: context, narrative, company connection, or framing the resume format couldn't carry. |
| 4 | Mostly additive. One or two sentences reference resume content but add enough context to be worth including. |
| 3 | Some meaningful overlap — a paragraph that summarizes the resume without adding much. The cover letter would survive without it, but it's not actively harmful. |
| 2 | A whole paragraph is a prose version of resume bullets. Reader gets the same information twice, less efficiently. |
| 1 | The cover letter is the resume in paragraph form. No new information, no new framing, no new story. |

**Test**: After reading the cover letter, does the HM know something they didn't know from the resume? If no, score is 2 or below.

---

### 4. Tone & Close (10%)

Does the tone match the company's culture? Is the close confident and specific, not passive or presumptuous?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 5 | Tone calibrated to company culture — direct and bold for startups, measured and evidence-led for large companies. Close names a specific interest or next step ("I'd welcome the chance to discuss how my experience in X maps to your roadmap on Y"). Not groveling, not entitled. |
| 4 | Good tone with appropriate close. Slightly generic close but not passive. |
| 3 | Tone is appropriate but not calibrated. Close is standard ("I look forward to hearing from you"). |
| 2 | Tone is off — too formal for a startup, too casual for a large company, or self-congratulatory. Close is passive. |
| 1 | "I hope to hear from you soon" or "I believe I would be a perfect fit." Closes that signal low effort or entitlement. |

**Red flags**: "I hope to hear from you." "I am confident I would be a great fit." "Thank you for your time and consideration." Any of these suggest the close was written on autopilot.

---

### 5. Format & Length Discipline (10%)

One page. Three paragraphs. No exceptions. Format discipline is itself a PM signal — the book calls the resume "a product." Same rule applies to the cover letter.

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 5 | Exactly one page. Three paragraphs, each with clear purpose. Correct formatting — paragraph breaks visible, no walls of text. Reads in under 60 seconds. |
| 4 | One page. Slightly unbalanced paragraphs (one too long) but total length is right. |
| 3 | One page but dense. Reader has to work to find the structure. |
| 2 | Running over one page, or one paragraph is so long it reads as a block of text. |
| 1 | Over one page. Or three lines total that don't constitute a real letter. |

---

## Scoring

```
Weighted total = (D1 × 0.30) + (D2 × 0.30) + (D3 × 0.20) + (D4 × 0.10) + (D5 × 0.10)
```

| Score | Assessment |
|-------|------------|
| Below 2.5 | Would not move to phone screen — too generic or repetitive |
| 2.5 – 3.0 | Borderline — doesn't hurt, doesn't help |
| 3.0 – 3.5 | Solid — reinforces the resume positively |
| 3.5 – 4.0 | Strong — HM reads it and thinks "this person did their homework" |
| 4.0+ | Exceptional — HM shares it as an example of a good cover letter |

---

## Common Failure Patterns

- **Generic hook**: Paraphrasing the company's website is not research — it's reading
- **Resume prose**: "As demonstrated by my experience at [Company], where I led X..." — cut, it's in the resume
- **Accomplishment scatter**: Listing 5+ accomplishments loses the reader — 2–3, well-chosen and JD-mapped, beats a comprehensive list
- **Passive close**: "I'd love the opportunity to discuss" — specific beats vague: "I'd welcome a conversation about how my work on [X] maps to your [JD priority]"
- **Wrong tone for company**: A formal cover letter sent to a Series B startup, or an informal one sent to Amazon — signals you didn't research the culture
- **Written before resume is approved**: Evidence paragraph uses framing inconsistent with the final resume bullets — creates contradictions the HM will notice
